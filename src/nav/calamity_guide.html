Typhoons

The Philippines is frequently hit by typhoons (tropical cyclones) due to its location in the Pacific Typhoon Belt. These storms bring strong winds, heavy rainfall, and storm surges, causing widespread flooding, landslides, and damage to infrastructure.

Typhoon: Stock Up on Food and Water

  1. Stock Up on Food and Water:

    • Choose canned food with long expiration dates.
    • Store enough water for three days; fill the bathtub if staying home.
    • Remember, an average person needs about one gallon of water per day.

  2. Prepare Fridge and Freezer:

    • Consume perishables first.
    • Fill fridge/freezer with bottled water and non-perishable items.
    • Freeze water bottles and make ice bags for longer cold retention.

  3. Ensure Prescription Medication:

    • Have an ample supply, especially during storm seasons.
    • Some insurers may not refill until nearly used up; plan accordingly.

  4. Gather Necessities:

    • Prepare for a week without electricity or running water.
    • Include battery-powered light sources, a manual can opener, first aid kit, NOAA-compatible radio, and hygiene products.
    • Print a medical aid guide for reference.

  5. Take Supplies if Evacuating:

    • Consider smaller portions of food and water for limited space during evacuation.
    • Include flares, physical maps, charging station locations (for electric cars), jumper cables, and extra gas.

  6. Regularly Check and Update Kit:

    • Ensure supplies are fresh and well-stocked.
    • Check airbeds for leaks and batteries for a full charge using a battery tester.
    • Maintain a checklist with categorized and dated items for easy monitoring.

Earthquakes

The Philippines is situated on the Pacific Ring of Fire, making it prone to seismic activities. Earthquakes can result in infrastructure damage, landslides, and tsunamis, especially in coastal areas.

Earthquake: Disaster Preparedness Plan

  1. Create a Disaster Preparedness Plan:

    • Identify safe spots in your building.
    • Teach signaling for help if trapped.
    • Practice the plan regularly.

  2. Practice "Drop, Cover, and Hold On":

    • Make it a habit in every room.
    • If outside, move to open areas away from potential hazards.

  3. Learn Basic First Aid and CPR:

    • Attend classes or use local resources.
    • Keep first aid books with emergency supplies.

  4. Choose a Family Rallying Point:

    • Away from buildings.
    • Have a backup plan if not everyone reaches it.
    • Identify out-of-area contacts for communication.

  5. Know How to Turn Off Utilities:

    • Especially the gas line to prevent explosions.

  6. Create and Share Emergency Contact Lists:

    • Include neighbors, landlord, and important medical information.
    • Emergency numbers for fire, medical, police, and insurance.

  7. Develop Routes for Getting Home:

    • Plan multiple routes due to potential obstructions.
    • Note dangerous structures and find alternative paths.

Volcanic Eruption

The Philippines has several active volcanoes. Eruptions can lead to ashfall, pyroclastic flows, and lava flows, causing evacuation of nearby communities and disrupting daily life.

Volcanic Eruption: Emergency Preparedness Plan

  1. Create an Emergency Communication Plan:

    • List all ways to contact family, including landline numbers.
    • Know emergency plans of schools, workplaces, and local government.
    • Designate an out-of-town contact for relaying information.

  2. Identify Meeting Points:

    • Choose four meeting places, including one at home or a nearby shelter.
    • Ensure accessibility for family members with disabilities and include plans for pets.
    • Designate places within the neighborhood, town, and outside of town.

  3. Discuss Plans with Family:

    • Share details with everyone and distribute relevant contact information.
    • Rehearse and revise plans at regular family meetings.
    • Address children's concerns by involving them in the planning process.

  4. Consider Financial Implications:

    • Explore insurance options for volcano-related damage.
    • Develop a business continuity plan if your business is in a high-risk area.
    • Prioritize the safety of both family and staff in emergency preparations.

Flooding

Heavy rainfall, often associated with typhoons, can lead to widespread flooding. Urban areas are particularly vulnerable due to poor drainage systems and deforestation.

Flood: Evacuation and Emergency Supplies

  1. Establish Evacuation Locations:

    • Plan meeting spots, like a friend's safe home or a local shelter.
    • Ensure everyone knows these locations and the high-ground routes.
    • Contact local authorities for designated shelters.

  2. Develop a Family Communications Plan:

    • Print FEMA's blank plans and fill in contact details and meeting spots.
    • Use texting during floods for better communication.

  3. Build an Emergency Supplies Kit:

    • Prepare a kit with three days' worth of food and water for everyone.
    • Include a week's supply of necessary medications and a first aid kit.
    • Check and replace food supplies annually.
    • Bring personal identification documents, extra cash, and store them in a waterproof container.
    • Tailor the kit to your family's needs, considering items like a can opener, duct tape, pet and baby supplies, and sanitary items.

Landslides

Steep terrain in some regions makes landslides a common occurrence, especially during heavy rains and typhoons. Deforestation and improper land use practices can exacerbate this problem.

Landslides: Safety Measures

  1. Clear the Slope:

    • Remove rocks, fallen trees, and debris from the top of slopes.
    • Consult professionals for heavy material removal.
    • Be mindful of neighbors when relocating debris.

  2. Avoid Piling Debris:

    • Keep loose debris at least 10 feet away from the slope.
    • Don't pile scrap wood, lawn cuttings, or spare parts near the edge.
    • Avoid dumping water at the slope to prevent soil erosion.

  3. Place Structures Safely:

    • Build sheds or structures away from the slope edge.
    • Consult an engineer for a safe distance based on soil, drainage, and slope.
    • No strict rule; depends on local conditions and landslide risk.

Tsunamis

Earthquakes, especially those occurring under the sea, can trigger tsunamis. Coastal areas are at risk of large waves that can inundate low-lying areas.

Tsunami: Evacuation and Emergency Kits

  1. Know Evacuation Routes:

    • Identify the quickest way to reach high ground.
    • Familiarize yourself with terrain and practice evacuation routes.
    • Make sure your family knows the plan.

  2. Create Emergency Kits:

    • Prepare kits for home, work, and vehicle with 72 hours' worth of supplies.
    • Include water, food, flashlight, radio, first aid, and personal items.
    • Consider specific needs for infants, elderly, etc.

  3. Establish Family Communication Plan:

    • Plan where to meet if family members are in different places.
    • Invest in walkie talkies for communication.
    • Familiarize yourself with school policies if you have kids.

  4. Take a First Aid Course:

    • Learn CPR and basic first aid skills.
    • Consider taking a first aid course from a school, hospital, or community center.

  5. Learn Survival Skills:

    • Acquire survival skills for various situations.
    • Share your knowledge with others and encourage community programs.

  6. Consider Flood Insurance:

    • Look into flood insurance, especially if close to the coast.
    • Consider investing in a hurricane shelter for added protection.

Droughts: Water Shortage Preparedness

Part 1: Prepare for Water Shortage

  1. Water Rationing Plan:

    • Plan for 1 gallon per person per day during water shortages.
    • Consider specific needs of children, nursing mothers, and those with illnesses.
    • Stock extra water for medical emergencies.
    • Communicate water usage limits with family.

  2. Stock Bottled Water:

    • Have at least a week's supply of bottled water.
    • Use bottled water as a last resort if tap water is unavailable.

  3. Rain Catch System:

    • Install a rainwater catchment system using large drums.
    • Place drums under downspout gutters or areas where water runs off.
    • Filter rainwater before drinking, preferably boiling it for three minutes.

  4. Part 2: Conserve Water in Your Household

    • Check and Repair Leaks:

      • Regularly check and repair leaks in faucets, handles, and toilets.
      • Use food coloring to identify leaks in toilet tanks.

    • Install Water-Efficient Appliances:

      • Upgrade to water-efficient appliances, such as low-flow showerheads and low-volume toilets.

    • Turn Water Off When Not in Use:

      • Develop a habit of turning off the faucet when not in use, especially when brushing teeth or shaving.

    • Reuse Water:

      • Collect water wasted while waiting for the faucet or shower to warm up.
      • Use collected water for plants or other purposes.

    • Water Your Lawn Sparingly:

      • Water lawns once a week during summer.
      • Set alarms to avoid over-watering and check local regulations on watering during droughts.
